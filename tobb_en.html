Are Financial Conditions a Determinant of the Impact of Monetary Policy Shocks on Asset Prices

Evidence From US

Mehmet Balcilar
mbalcilar@newhaven.edu

University of New Haven

August 6, 2023

Introduction

Background (1/2)

  • Asset price reactions to monetary shocks vary with financial conditions.
    • Monetary policy shocks may produce muted responses during tranquil times but become highly potent when financial markets are under stress, due to amplifying mechanisms such as constrained credit supply, elevated risk aversion, and disrupted expectations.
  • Swanson (2021) identifies three distinct policy instruments:
    • Federal Funds Rate (FFR): affects short-term interest rates and cost of capital.
    • Forward Guidance (FG): influences expectations about the future path of rates.
    • Large-Scale Asset Purchases (LSAPs): compress long-term yields through demand pressure and portfolio rebalancing.

Background (2/2)

  • Alessandri & Mumtaz (2017, 2019): Financial regimes—defined by tight or loose financial conditions—modify the propagation of shocks.
    • In tight regimes, frictions and nonlinearities play a greater role in amplifying even modest shocks.

Motivation

  • Policy effectiveness is not invariant to the state of financial markets.
    • During periods of elevated financial stress, the traditional transmission mechanisms (e.g., interest rate channel) may be impaired, while unconventional tools like LSAPs gain traction.
  • Financial frictions, when binding, introduce asymmetries in how monetary impulses affect asset prices and real activity.
    • Theoretically grounded in models such as Bernanke, Gertler & Gilchrist (1999) and Brunnermeier & Sannikov (2014).
  • The key empirical question How do the effects of monetary shocks differ across financial regimes? Which policy instruments retain potency under distress?

Understanding regime-dependent effects is essential for designing resilient monetary policy strategies.

Summary of This Paper

  • Empirically quantifies asset price responses to different types of monetary policy shocks under tight and loose financial conditions.
    • Focuses on equity prices and house prices, which are both highly sensitive to interest rates and risk conditions.
  • Utilizes a threshold VAR framework that endogenously classifies financial regimes based on the National Financial Conditions Index (NFCI).
  • Identification strategy exploits high-frequency monetary policy surprises around FOMC announcements.
    • Allows for clean separation of exogenous monetary shocks from endogenous macro-financial noise.
  • Finds stronger asset price responses to LSAP and FFR shocks under tight financial conditions, while forward guidance is relatively ineffective in both regimes.

Data

National Financial Conditions Index (NFCI) (1/2)

  • Constructed by the Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago, NFCI provides a weekly measure of U.S. financial conditions.
    • Based on over 100 indicators covering money markets, credit spreads, leverage, and equity volatility.
  • Mixed-frequency dynamic factor model aggregates information into a single standardized index.
    • Positive values indicate tighter-than-average conditions; negative values denote looser conditions.
    • Subcomponents (risk, credit, leverage) help decompose sources of stress.

NFCI acts as both a contemporaneous barometer and a leading indicator of financial stress.

National Financial Conditions Index (NFCI) (2/2)

Monetary Policy Shocks (1/5)

  • Following Swanson (2021), shocks are derived from market surprises in tight windows around FOMC announcements.
    • Surprise = change in asset prices (e.g., futures or bond yields) immediately after policy release.

Monetary Policy Shocks (2/5)

  • Three categories:
    • FFR shocks: Changes in 1–3 month Fed Funds Futures rates.
    • FG shocks: Changes in Eurodollar/OIS (Overnight Index Swap) futures rates at 1-2 year horizon, capturing forward guidance.
    • LSAP shocks: Changes in 10-year Treasury yields orthogonal to short rate expectations.
  • These shocks serve as external instruments in VAR framework.
    • Allow for causal identification of structural impulse responses.

This identification strategy mitigates simultaneity bias by exploiting narrow windows of policy news.

Methodology

Model: Bayesian (Self-Exciting) Structural Threshold Vector Autoregression (BSETSVAR) (1/2)

  • The empirical model is a threshold (regime-switching) vector autoregression (TVAR), estimated in Bayesian framework.
    • Allows coefficients and shock responses to vary depending on financial conditions.

Let \(X_{t} = (X_{1t},X_{2t},\ldots,X_{nt})^\prime\) be \(n \times 1\) vector of time series.

BSETSVAR

\[ X_{t} = \left( v_{1} + \sum_{j = 1}^{p}{A_{1,j}X_{t - j}} + \Omega_{1}^{1/2}e_{1,t} \right)S_{t} + \left( v_{2} + \sum_{j = 1}^{p}{A_{2,j}X_{t - j}} + \Omega_{2}^{1/2}e_{2,t} \right){(1 - S}_{t}) \]

  • Regimes are determined by lagged NFCI (\(F_{t-d}\)), with threshold \(c^{*}\) estimated from the data.

Model (2/2)

  • State variable \(S_{t}\) signifies the embedded indicator function
  • Distinguish normal and financially stressful times.
  • Regimes determined by the level of the US financial condition index (\(F\)) relative to an unobserved threshold value, \(c^{*}\):

State Variable

\[ S_{t} = 1 \Longleftrightarrow F_{t - d} \leq c^{*} \qquad \text{and} \qquad S_{t} = 0 \Longleftrightarrow F_{t - d} > c^{*} \]

where the delay \(d\) and the threshold \(c^{*}\) parameters are unknown.

BSETSVAR

\[ X_t = \begin{cases} v_{1} + \sum_{j = 1}^{p}{A_{1,j}X_{t - j}} + \Omega_{1}^{1/2}e_{1,t}, & F_{t - d} \leq c^{*} \\ v_{2} + \sum_{j = 1}^{p}{A_{2,j}X_{t - j}} + \Omega_{2}^{1/2}e_{2,t}, & F_{t - d} > c^{*} \end{cases} \]

Model Variables and Features

  • \(Y_t\): Vector of endogenous variables
    • Federal Funds Rate (FFR)
    • Equity Prices (S&P 500, log-level)
    • Dividends (real)
    • House Prices (Case-Shiller index, real)
    • CPI inflation (log difference)
    • Industrial Production (log difference)
  • Period 1991:M1-2019:M6 with 336 observations
  • Lag structure selected by BIC; typically 2–4 lags.
  • Priors: Minnesota-type shrinkage, regime-invariant across equations.
  • Switching driven by lagged NFCI (threshold variable), estimated within the model.
  • Time-varying impulse response functions (IRFs) computed separately by regime.

External Instruments (Proxy SVAR)

  • Structural shocks \(\varepsilon_{jt}\) are identified using external instruments—market surprises \(m_t\)—as proposed in Mertens & Ravn (2013).

Instrument Validity

\[ \mathbb{E}[m_t \varepsilon_{1t}] \neq 0, \quad \mathbb{E}[m_t \varepsilon_{jt}] = 0 \quad \text{for } j \neq 1 \]

  • Estimation proceeds in two steps:
    • First-stage: Regress VAR residuals on instruments to extract structural shock series.
    • Second-stage: Estimate impulse responses based on identified shock.
  • Advantage: Allows credible identification without strong ordering assumptions.

This strategy uncovers the causal impact of specific policy tools on financial markets.

Estimation and Inference

  • Bayesian estimation via Gibbs sampling, with priors on lag decay and variance structures.
  • Threshold and delay parameters are inferred using marginal likelihood.
  • Separate sets of IRFs are computed for each regime.
    • Posterior medians + 68% credible intervals shown in figures.

Regime-specific IRFs quantify how monetary transmission varies across financial states.

Empirical Results

Impulse Responses to FFR Shocks

  • Equity and house prices fall more in tight conditions.
    • This aligns with constrained arbitrage and deleveraging mechanisms.
  • Real economy reacts with delay but amplified during stress.
    • Investment and consumption are more sensitive when financing conditions are tight.

Traditional rate policy is more powerful under stress — opposite of the usual ZLB logic.

Impulse Responses to Forward Guidance

  • FG less effective in distress periods.
    • Possibly due to credibility issues or impaired expectations channel.
  • Asset price effects are muted vs. FFR or LSAPs.
    • Equities barely respond; house prices show minimal change.

Impulse Responses to LSAPs

  • Strong asset price reactions in both regimes, especially during tight conditions.
    • Reflects improved liquidity, term premia compression, and portfolio balance.
  • Amplification suggests financial frictions are critical.
    • LSAPs may alleviate risk constraints faced by intermediaries.

Comparison Across Instruments

  • LSAPs dominate in magnitude during distress.
    • These effects rival or exceed those of FFR in stressed states.
  • FG consistently weak, even in loose conditions.
    • Questions the centrality of forward guidance as a primary tool.

Robustness Checks

  • Results hold under alternative identification and threshold specifications.
    • Varying delay parameter \(d\) and using HP-filtered FCI do not alter key insights.

Conclusion and Policy Implications

Summary

  • Monetary policy effects on asset prices are state-dependent.
    • Ignoring financial regimes masks important asymmetries.
  • LSAPs are particularly effective under financial distress.
    • Supports balance sheet interventions during crises.
  • FG appears to have limited transmission power in such times.
    • Suggests limits to purely expectations-based interventions.

Implications

  • Policymakers should condition interventions on financial regime.
    • Real-time FCI metrics can help guide policy mix.
  • During crises, balance sheet tools may outperform traditional rate cuts.
    • Suggests stronger role for QE, particularly in housing markets.
  • Forward guidance alone insufficient when credit constraints bind.
    • Emphasizes importance of liquidity channels over expectations channels.
